What are red flags?

Red flags are warning signs of unhealthy, manipulative, or abusive behaviors in relationships. They’re often subtle at first, but noticing them early can help keep you safe.

Classic Red Flags

  • Jealousy disguised as concern: “I just love you so much, I can’t stand to see you talk to anyone else.”
  • Quick involvement / Love bombing: Intense affection or gifts early on, pressuring commitment.
  • Isolating you from friends/family: “You don’t need them, you have me.”
  • Controlling behavior: Dictating what you wear, who you see, or how you spend your money/time.
  • Constant criticism or put-downs: Belittling your looks, intelligence, or interests—“just joking” or not.
  • Blaming you for their actions: “If you hadn’t made me mad, I wouldn’t have yelled.”
  • Explosive temper or mood swings: You feel like you’re walking on eggshells.
  • History of bad relationships: “My exes were all crazy.”
  • Disregard for your boundaries: Pressuring you for sex, commitment, or to “just let it go.”
  • Gaslighting: Denying things you know happened, making you doubt your own memory or sanity.
  • Minimizing or excusing hurtful behavior: “You’re too sensitive.”
  • Threatening self-harm if you leave: Weaponizing their own well-being to keep you stuck.
  • Financial control: Taking your money, restricting access to finances, or making you justify every purchase.

Lesser-Known Red Flags

  • Too much, too soon: Planning your life together on date three, constant texting/check-ins.
  • Backhanded compliments: “You’d be so pretty if you lost weight.”
  • Testing your loyalty: Creating drama or jealousy traps to “see how you react.”
  • Refusing responsibility: “It’s just how I am, because of my past.”
  • Disrespecting your NO: Not taking “no” for an answer, pushing boundaries “as a joke.”
  • Keeping secrets: Sudden phone privacy, vague about past or daily life.
  • Your gut says something is off: Even if you can’t explain why—trust it!

Why Do People Ignore Red Flags?

  • Fear of being alone
  • Belief that love means “fixing” someone
  • Minimizing their own needs
  • Confusing drama with passion
  • Past trauma or normalizing bad behavior
  • Hope that things will change

Green Flags: Signs of a Healthy Relationship

  • Open, honest communication: You can talk about feelings, needs, and concerns without fear of ridicule or retaliation.
  • Respect for boundaries: Your “no” is accepted without guilt trips, pressure, or punishment.
  • Support for your independence: You’re encouraged to have your own friends, interests, and goals.
  • Accountability: Both people can apologize, take responsibility, and work on their mistakes.
  • Trust: You don’t feel like you have to prove your loyalty, and jealousy isn’t a daily issue.
  • Equality: Decisions, responsibilities, and emotional labor are shared—not dumped on one person.
  • Kindness and empathy: You feel heard, cared for, and valued—even in disagreements.
  • Healthy conflict: Arguments don’t get cruel or violent, and solutions are found together.
  • Safety: You feel physically, emotionally, and mentally safe—never threatened or controlled.
  • Celebrating each other’s successes: Wins are celebrated, not minimized or resented.
  • Shared values and growth: You can grow together, learn together, and change is welcomed, not feared.

Green flags aren’t just “nice to have”—they’re the foundation of a healthy relationship. You deserve them all.

How to Build Green Flags in Your Relationship

  • Practice open communication:
    Get curious instead of defensive. Share your feelings honestly (“I feel…” not “You always…”), and listen without planning your rebuttal.
  • Respect boundaries—yours and theirs:
    Ask what’s comfortable. If someone says “no,” thank them for telling you. If you need space, state it gently but firmly.
  • Encourage independence:
    Support each other’s friendships, hobbies, and solo time. Cheer them on! Don’t make everything about “us.”
  • Take accountability:
    Own your mistakes. “I was wrong and I’m sorry.” No “but you…” or deflecting. This builds trust fast.
  • Foster trust:
    Be consistent and reliable. Keep promises, avoid secret-keeping, and talk through jealousy instead of letting it fester.
  • Promote equality:
    Share decision-making and household tasks. Don’t assume one person will “just do it.” Ask, don’t assign.
  • Lead with kindness and empathy:
    Pause before reacting. Imagine their perspective. Little acts of care and understanding go a long way.
  • Learn healthy conflict skills:
    Take breaks when emotions run high, use “I” statements, and look for win-win solutions. It’s you two vs. the problem—not you vs. each other.
  • Prioritize safety:
    Agree that violence, threats, and manipulation are never okay. Create a safe space for honest conversations, even about hard stuff.
  • Celebrate and support growth:
    Notice progress, big or small. Celebrate wins together and encourage each other’s goals—even if they’re different!

Remember: No one is born knowing how to do all of this. Healthy habits are built with practice, patience, and self-compassion.

Try This: Pick one tip above and practice it this week. Notice how it feels—for you and your relationships. Progress, not perfection!
